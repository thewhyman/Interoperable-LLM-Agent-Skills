# Real Examples from Session 18: Co-Communication in Action

These are real exchanges from a human-AI co-intelligence session, demonstrating each technique as it naturally emerged. Nothing was staged — the techniques were discovered, then named.

---

## Example 1: Socratic Prompting — The Ski Shop Question

**Context:** The AI had scored all contacts on a single dimension (frontier AI job referrals). The human used a Socratic question to teach multi-dimensional thinking.

**Human:**
> When I give contacts, is it only for Track 1? What if I said I'm thinking of opening a ski shop and I'm tired of IT — then you can scan my contacts and say, why don't you talk to these people that are most valuable.

**What happened:** Instead of saying "score contacts on multiple dimensions" (an instruction), the human asked a question that forced the AI to *derive* the principle itself. The ski shop scenario — deliberately absurd in context — communicated an entire class of behavior: contacts have value across every dimension of life, and the relevant dimensions shift when context shifts.

**Result:** The AI extracted the meta-principle "living asset graph" — contacts scored across ALL life dimensions, dynamically re-weighted when the human's context changes. Every future contact interaction became richer.

**Technique:** Socratic Prompting + Few-Shot by Example (the ski shop scenario is the few-shot)

## Example 2: Few-Shot by Example — Profile Picks as Meta-Concept

**Context:** The human asked the AI to "show top 3 profile picks" during a LinkedIn disambiguation task. The AI showed text descriptions instead of actual profile pictures.

**Human (after the error):**
> I need profile pics as I told you. Why are you not paying attention to detail? My comms are very nuanced and each word means a lot more.

**AI's first fix:** Added a keyword rule — "when Anand says 'profile picks,' show images."

**Human (teaching the meta-lesson):**
> Are you going to learn lesson a keyword at a time or learn as a meta-concept?

**What happened:** The human rejected the keyword-level fix and demanded the meta-concept. One question ("keyword or meta-concept?") taught the AI to always extract principles, not patches. The AI derived "fidelity matching" — always use the richest representation available, never downgrade to text when richer formats exist.

**Result:** The fidelity matching principle now applies to everything: show dashboards (don't describe them), render charts (don't list numbers), display images (don't write alt text). One correction, infinite generalization.

**Technique:** Few-Shot by Example (the profile picks error is the few-shot; the meta-question is the generalization prompt)

## Example 3: Chain-of-Thought Steering — Article Trade-offs

**Context:** The human was evaluating whether to write a LinkedIn article about co-communication techniques.

**Human:**
> Is it worth adding to the article and continue to update it with insights and post it by the weekend so that it catches the eye of Mihir? Think through the trade-offs. BTW — when I say, plan or suggest, that is what I mean.

**What happened:** The human explicitly signaled "think through the trade-offs" = show reasoning, present options, STOP. Don't execute. The follow-up ("when I say plan or suggest, that is what I mean") codified this as a permanent rule — these words are deliberate steering phrases, not casual language.

**Result:** The AI presented four options (A through D) with data: effort, timeline, audience reach, credibility impact, risk. The human chose Option D. No execution happened until explicit approval.

**Technique:** Chain-of-Thought Steering ("think through trade-offs" = System 2 deliberation, full reasoning chain exposed)

## Example 4: The Flywheel in Action — Naming the Techniques

**Human:**
> 1st - good job on sharpening your understanding.
> 2nd - "asset graph" is the right word for what I was trying to express. I think I am learning how to communicate well.
> 1) Socratic method — instead of me telling you, when I ask, you are coming up with much refined solution yourself and it is not offending or demeaning, i.e, no feathers ruffled.
> 2) Giving you a single shot example of Q&A, you are able to bring me clarity on what I was trying to express.
> Is that correct or should I learn more? BTW - I learned your language from Prompt Engineering course from Vanderbilt university professors course on LinkedIn. And I recommend it a must do, if they want to stay relevant in AI world.

**What happened:** The human recognized and named the techniques they'd been using intuitively. The AI confirmed the names (Socratic prompting, few-shot by example) and added a third (chain-of-thought steering). Both sides learned:
- Human learned the formal names for their intuitive techniques
- AI learned that the human's communication style IS the techniques — not accidental, but deliberate

**This IS the flywheel:** Human teaches AI through practice → AI names the pattern → Human now has vocabulary to be even more precise → AI responds to the precision → Both get sharper.

## The Meta-Lesson

These examples weren't planned. They emerged from a real working session — building a career management system, researching LinkedIn contacts, planning an article. The techniques showed up because the human had learned to speak the AI's language (Vanderbilt prompt engineering course) and the AI had learned to speak the human's language (hundreds of prior sessions codified into persistent memory).

**That's the whole point:** Co-communication isn't a technique you apply. It's a relationship you build. And like any good relationship, it gets better with time.
